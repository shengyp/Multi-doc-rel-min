Coherence and Salience-Based Multi-Document Relationship Mining


Yongpan Sheng1 Zenglin Xu1 Chaoran Huang2 Gerard de Melo3

1University of Electronic Science and Technology of China, China
2University of Science and Technology of China,China 2University of New South Wales, Australia



1. Abstract

In today's interconnected world, there is an endless 24/7 stream of new articles appearing online. Faced with these overwhelming amounts of data, it is often helpful to consider only the key entities and concepts and their relationships. This is challenging, as relevant connections may be spread across a number of disparate articles and sources. In this paper, we propose a unified framework to aid users in quickly discerning salient connections and facts from a set of related documents, and presents the resulting information in a graph-based visualization. Specifically, given a set of documents as input, first, we extract candidate facts from above sources by leveraging Open Information Extraction (Open IE) approaches. Then, we design a Two-Stage Candidate Triple Filtering (TCTF) approach to maintain only coherent facts associated with the specified document topic from the candidates and connect them in form of an initial graph. We also present a heuristic to obtain a final conceptual graph based on the graph in which the salient entities and concepts included. Our graph visualization then allows users to explore these connections. The extensive experiments on two real-world data sets illustrate that our approach achieves 8.1\% higher F-score over state-of-the-art open RE methods. Sim(f, l) = count sim (f, l)/count(l)

where countsim(f, l) denotes the number of similar candidates of f in candidates l, count(l) is the total number of candidates. The similarity scores between two candidate fact fi,fj is computed as sim (fi,fj) = γs˙k + (1 − γ)lk , where sk,lk denote the semantic similarity and literal similarity scores between the facts, respectively. We compute sk using the Align, Disambiguate and Walk algorithm 5,while lk are computed using the Jaccard index. γ = 0.8 denotes the relative degree to which the semantic similarity contributes to the overall similarity score, as opposed to the literal similarity.

3. Case Study

3.1 Candidate Facts Extraction

Algebraic problem solving is one of the most important modules of elementary mathematics. Its main feature is that mathematical problems contain rigorous problem solving logic and complex symbolic calculations. At the same time, algebra problems are very skillful, and they are often a type of problem. Changing the value will change the way of calculation. Therefore, in order to solve the algebra problem, we must accumulate relevant problem-solving skills in addition to the common knowledge points. Only in this way can we solve algebraic problems with diversity. Therefore, we have chosen this technical and computational subject.

The above examples are presented in terms of natural language understanding and human-like problem solving. First, we enter the question number to get the specific topic content. Secondly, the natural language processing module analyzes and understands the topics and conclusions and translates them into corresponding knowledge relationships. This forms the three relationships of CommonQustionRelations, SubstemRelation and SubConclusionRelation in the Question Relations table, each of which contains one or more specific knowledge relationships. Then, the reasoning module receives the result of the natural language processing. Finally stop the reasoning and output the result. This forms a complete solution process.

3.2 Geometry Example

The main key to answering the geometric problem lies in its rigorous logic. The conclusion of the latter step is derived from the conditions of the previous step. This must be supported by sufficient evidence. The conclusions to be proved or solved from the problem to the end must have a very rigorous derivation process. This aspect requires a large number of formulas and theorems, and on the other hand we have the ability to use them flexibly. Only in this way can we solve such problems. Here is an Olympic math problem for 2D geometry.
